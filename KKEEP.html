Official Retrospective Report

The Nijmegen Rain Timing Prediction Championship was conducted on 24 May 2026 across multiple designated observation points throughout the city of Nijmegen, Netherlands. The event brought together ten participants from diverse professional and academic backgrounds to engage in structured forecasting under real-world meteorological uncertainty.

Competitors were evaluated on their capacity to predict the precise onset of measurable rainfall — defined as ≥ 0.2 mm/hour — within timed prediction windows across sequential competition rounds.

The competition is issued under the institutional auspices of Atlas Nederlands Meteorologisch Instituut and governed by the Official Rulebook (Retrospective Edition).

Competition Rulebook

Nijmegen Rain Timing Prediction Championship · 24 May 2026 · Issued under Atlas Nederlands Meteorologisch Instituut

1. Introduction

The Nijmegen Rain Timing Prediction Championship is a structured forecasting competition designed to evaluate human precision in predicting the exact onset of measurable rainfall under real-world meteorological uncertainty.

The event was conducted on 24 May 2026 across multiple designated observation points in Nijmegen, Netherlands. Participants engaged in timed prediction rounds under naturally occurring weather variability, with outcomes determined exclusively by real-time precipitation data recorded by official measurement systems.

The competition reframes weather forecasting as a precision-based analytical discipline rather than passive interpretation of probabilistic forecasts.

2. Competition Format

The championship consisted of sequential prediction rounds conducted throughout the event day. Each round followed a fixed structure consisting of observation, prediction lock-in, monitoring, and verification phases.

Participants were required to submit predictions prior to each lock-in deadline. Once submitted, predictions were final and could not be altered under any circumstance.

Each round concluded upon detection of measurable rainfall at official sensor thresholds (≥ 0.2 mm/hour).

3. Observation and Prediction Procedure

During each round, participants were stationed at designated observation points across Nijmegen. These locations were selected to reflect varying microclimatic conditions within the urban environment.

Permitted Forecasting Resources

  • Publicly available weather forecasts and meteorological services
  • Direct environmental observation and personal sensory assessment
  • Personal meteorological interpretation tools and instruments

All predictions were required to be submitted within a fixed observation window prior to lock-in. No retrospective adjustments were permitted under any circumstance.

4. Round Structure

Each competition round consisted of four defined sequential phases:

Phase 01
Observation Phase
Environmental assessment and interpretation of available meteorological forecast data.
Duration: 10 minutes
Phase 02
Lock-In Phase
Submission of final predictions. No revisions permitted under any circumstances following lock-in.
Duration: Fixed deadline
Phase 03
Monitoring Phase
Passive observation period. Participants await precipitation onset at designated observation stations.
Duration: Variable
Phase 04
Trigger Phase
Official rainfall detection and round closure. Scores calculated based on deviation from actual onset time.
Duration: Upon detection

5. Scoring System

Performance was evaluated based on temporal accuracy and prediction alignment with observed meteorological events. Full scoring details are available on the Scoring System page.

  • Exact minute prediction: 100 points
  • ±1–2 minute deviation: 80 points
  • ±3–5 minute deviation: 50 points
  • Correct general time window: 20 points
  • Incorrect prediction: 0 points
  • Correct rainfall intensity prediction (bonus): +20 points
  • Correct "no rainfall" prediction (dry round bonus): +30 points

6. Competition Environment

The event was conducted under natural atmospheric conditions without artificial weather modification. Participants operated in open-air observation zones located throughout Nijmegen, including:

  • River-adjacent areas and Waalbridge sector observation points
  • Central urban squares and civic plaza zones
  • Elevated bridge viewpoints with extended atmospheric sightlines
  • Kronenburgerpark and urban green corridor positions

Environmental variability, including wind shifts and localized precipitation patterns, formed an integral part of the competitive structure. No participant was permitted to relocate observation zones following the lock-in phase.

7. Ranking and Classification

Participants were ranked cumulatively across all completed rounds. Final standings were determined by total aggregated points across the full event day.

  • Bronze Predictor Tier
  • Advanced Rain Analyst Tier
  • Precision Meteorology Tier
  • Grandmaster of Precipitation Timing

8. Championship Outcome

At the conclusion of the final round, participants were ranked according to total predictive accuracy. The highest-scoring participant was awarded the championship title and the following distinction:

The Golden Drop of Nijmegen Award for Temporal Rainfall Precision

Awarded to the participant demonstrating the highest cumulative predictive accuracy across all competition rounds. The 2026 edition was claimed by Umut Aydan with a total score of 657 points — the first recipient in championship history.

9. Conceptual Framework

The Nijmegen Rain Timing Prediction Championship operates within a broader conceptual framework examining human interpretive accuracy under probabilistic environmental systems.

Rather than competing against weather itself, participants engage with the limits of prediction, timing, and uncertainty resolution in real-time atmospheric conditions. The competition is formally supported by Atlas Nederlands Meteorologisch Instituut as the official institutional sponsor.

Official Methodology

Scoring System

Point allocation, bonus criteria, and classification thresholds · NRTPC 2026
Evaluation Criteria
Temporal Accuracy Point Allocation

All scores are derived from the deviation between a participant's predicted rainfall onset time and the officially recorded detection time at the relevant sensor station. Scoring is applied independently to each competition round.

Primary Point Allocation

Applied per round based on temporal deviation from actual onset

Exact minute — zero deviation 100 pts
±1–2 minute deviation 80 pts
±3–5 minute deviation 50 pts
Correct general time window 20 pts
Incorrect prediction (outside window) 0 pts

Bonus Point Modifiers

Awarded in addition to the primary score where applicable

Correct rainfall intensity classification +20 pts
Correct "no rainfall" prediction (dry round) +30 pts

Bonus points are applied cumulatively. A participant achieving both the exact-minute prediction and a correct intensity classification in the same round would score a maximum of 120 points per round.

Classification Tier Structure

Grandmaster of Precipitation Timing
Awarded to the championship winner. Highest cumulative score across all competition rounds. Recipient of the Golden Drop of Nijmegen Award for Temporal Rainfall Precision.
Precision Meteorology Tier
Participants demonstrating consistently high predictive accuracy across multiple rounds. Strong finish in the upper rankings.
Advanced Rain Analyst Tier
Participants achieving solid mid-field performance with measurable meteorological interpretive skill across the competition rounds.
Bronze Predictor Tier
Participants who completed all competition rounds and registered valid predictions. Entry-level classification recognising full participation.
Measurement Standard

Measurable rainfall is defined as precipitation exceeding a sensor threshold of ≥ 0.2 mm per hour at the designated official measurement point for the relevant competition round. All timing records are derived from calibrated meteorological instrumentation operated under Atlas Nederlands Meteorologisch Instituut protocols. Participant-reported times are not accepted as official evidence under any circumstances.

Conceptual Foundation

About the Championship

Philosophy, methodology, and institutional context · NRTPC 2026

Human Prediction Under Atmospheric Uncertainty

The Nijmegen Rain Timing Prediction Championship emerges from a straightforward but underexplored question: how precisely can a human being identify the moment rain will begin? Not approximately, not in broad meteorological terms — but to the exact minute, in a named location, under real and uncontrolled atmospheric conditions.

Most conventional engagement with weather forecasting is passive and probabilistic. People consult percentage-based forecasts and make loose behavioural adjustments. The NRTPC inverts this arrangement: it asks participants to commit, precisely and irrevocably, to a specific prediction — and then to be held accountable for that commitment against objective reality.

In this sense, the championship is less about weather itself and more about the nature of interpretive certainty under conditions of genuine uncertainty.

Weather Forecasting as Interpretive Discipline

The competitive structure of the NRTPC treats meteorological interpretation as a skill — one that can be practised, refined, and evaluated. Participants are not simply consumers of forecast information; they are active interpreters of atmospheric data, integrating environmental observation, probabilistic forecast outputs, and personal analytical frameworks into a single, locked prediction.

The event reveals the gap between what we know about weather systems and what we can actually predict with precision. It makes that gap visible, measurable, and — within the structured context of competition — consequential.

Competitors come from varied professional and academic backgrounds. This is by design. A bridge maintenance worker and a physics student bring different interpretive resources to the same atmospheric conditions. The championship examines what kinds of knowledge — tacit, local, academic, experiential — most effectively resolve meteorological uncertainty.

The Satirical Civic Frame

The NRTPC operates under a deliberately serious institutional aesthetic. The rulebook is formal. The scoring system is precise. The award title — the Golden Drop of Nijmegen Award for Temporal Rainfall Precision — is entirely earnest in its absurdity. Classification tiers are named. A sponsor has been appointed.

This framing is not incidental. By treating an essentially uncontrollable meteorological phenomenon with the formal apparatus of competitive sport and civic administration, the championship draws attention to the bureaucratic optimism embedded in so many institutional efforts to manage natural systems.

We write policy. We build models. We issue predictions with decimal-point precision. Rain, however, does not read our documents. The NRTPC is, in part, a meditation on that relationship.

Institutional Sponsorship

The 2026 championship was conducted under the official auspices of Atlas Nederlands Meteorologisch Instituut, whose institutional support lent the event both its formal credibility and its meteorological legitimacy. The institute's involvement reflects a genuine interest in examining the boundaries of human predictive capacity within real atmospheric conditions.

Atlas Nederlands has provided measurement infrastructure, official sensor thresholds, and timing verification protocols for all competition rounds. Their contribution is acknowledged with gratitude by the organizing committee.
